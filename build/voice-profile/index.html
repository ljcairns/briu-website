How We Built a Voice Profile From 34,000 Lines of Writing

Your agent drafts emails, writes website copy, and talks to customers on your behalf. If it doesn't sound like you, none of it works. Here's how we built a voice profile that catches the difference between "correct" and "right."

The Problem

AI-generated content has a voice. It's polished, safe, and sounds like every other AI-generated content on the internet. Em dashes everywhere. "I'd love to" and "I'm excited to" in every email. Paragraphs that restate what the previous paragraph just said. No contractions in places where any real person would use them.

When your agent drafts a client email and it reads like ChatGPT wrote it, the client notices. Maybe not consciously. But the trust erodes. The whole point of an agent handling your communication is that it sounds like you handled it. If it doesn't, you're just adding a review step to content you'll rewrite anyway.

We needed a system that captures how Lucas actually writes, not how AI thinks a professional should write, and makes it available to every agent interaction, every draft, every piece of website copy.

We pulled from every source of real writing we could find. 34,201 lines across five sources.

What We Found

The analysis surfaced patterns that no style guide would have predicted. These aren't rules someone decided to follow. They're habits that showed up consistently across 34,000 lines.

Signature patterns (use these)

Parenthetical Precision

Context lives in parentheses: "(up from 4.5 in January)", "(56% chat-to-click rate)", "(a VPN)". Showed up in emails, business plans, and Discord messages. The single strongest identifier.

Front-Loaded Takeaways

The first sentence is always the point. "Key takeaway from February: quality of engagement continues to improve." Then the supporting detail. Never the other way around.

Data Over Claims

Never "powerful" or "industry-leading." Always the number: "96% of injection attacks caught." Percentages always paired with raw counts: "111 clicks (56% chat-to-click rate)."

Contractions, Always

we've, we'll, it's, don't, I'm, I'd. Across every source, every context. The only exceptions were deliberate weight in formal documents.

"That said" for Pivots

Not "however." Not "on the other hand." Always "That said," to introduce counterpoints or caveats.

"Happy to [verb]"

"Happy to walk through the details." "Happy to connect for a brief call." Never "I'd love to" or "I'd be happy to discuss."

Forbidden patterns (the AI fingerprint)

These are the patterns that showed up zero times across 34,000 lines of real writing but appear constantly in AI-generated content:

  • "I hope this email finds you well" (or any variant)
  • "I'd love to" / "I'm excited to" / "thrilled to" / "We're delighted"
  • "Just circling back" / "Just checking in"
  • "leverage" (as verb) / "synergy" / "cutting-edge" / "game-changing" / "next-generation"
  • "Best regards" / "Kind regards" / "Warm regards" / "Sincerely"
  • Em dashes. Lucas uses commas, periods, or parentheses. Never em dashes.
  • Recap paragraphs that restate what was just said
  • Paragraphs that exist only to justify the value of what was just described

The 2024 vs. 2026 discovery

The analysis caught something we didn't expect. Lucas's 2024 writing sounds different from his 2026 writing. Earlier emails had phrases like "We greatly appreciate you and your partnership!" and "Looking forward to helping you drive even more value!" Those were AI-assisted drafts from a period when the tools were doing more of the writing.

By 2026, the voice tightened significantly. Shorter sentences, more direct, no performative language. If content sounds like 2024-era Lucas, it's wrong. The voice profile needed to exclude those patterns explicitly, or the agent would learn the wrong version.

The casual voice (the secret weapon)

Discord messages and Claude Code sessions revealed patterns that never show up in polished writing but define personality:

  • Three modes of communication: Terse commands ("health check", "commit this"), voice-stream thinking (long unpunctuated runs exploring options), and structured specs (numbered lists with exact file paths).
  • Near-silent approval: "Good" followed by the next task. Never "great job" or "perfect."
  • Decision language: "lets do it" / "yeah lets." Casual imperative. Big decisions and small decisions get the same treatment.
  • "lol" as pressure release. Flat emotional register. Points out errors directly, never dwells, immediately pivots to fixing.

This matters because the agent doesn't just write emails. It communicates through Discord, drafts internal notes, and generates working documents. Knowing that Lucas says "Good" and moves on (not "Great work on this!") changes how the agent reports its own results.

The Two-File Architecture

A comprehensive voice profile is ~280 lines. That's too much context to inject into every drafting prompt, but too valuable to skip. We split it into two files optimized for different uses.

VOICE.md

~120 lines, compact operational guide

Read once per session, held in context for all drafting. Contains:

  • Voice DNA: 10 distinctive patterns with examples
  • Tone by Context: table mapping 7 contexts to register and rules
  • Personality Markers: 7 traits with examples
  • Forbidden Patterns: complete anti-AI checklist
  • 5 key examples covering the most common drafting scenarios

VOICE-FULL.md

~280 lines, complete reference

Read once per session for deep context. Referenced when drafting unfamiliar content types. Contains everything in VOICE.md plus:

  • Full email mechanics (openings, closings, CTAs)
  • Complete vocabulary analysis (register, jargon adaptation)
  • Audience adaptation for 6 different contexts
  • Casual working voice analysis (direction style, reactions, frustration)
  • 12 complete examples spanning every content type

The split matters for token efficiency. Most drafting sessions only need VOICE.md. When the agent encounters a content type it hasn't drafted before (a pitch deck, a complaint letter, a technical explainer for a non-technical audience), it pulls from VOICE-FULL.md for the specific patterns.

The Website Audit

Once the voice profile was built, we ran it against our own website. The results were not subtle.

25+ em dashes across the site. Lucas doesn't use em dashes. Every one was either a comma, a period, or a parenthetical trying to happen.

30+ missing contractions on the /why-now/ page alone. "It is not" instead of "it isn't." "You do not" instead of "you don't." The page read like a formal essay, not like the founder wrote it.

Consultant jargon: "orchestrate" (5 instances), "highest-leverage" (3 instances), "executive working session" (2 instances). Lucas says "run," "operate," and "coordinate."

Restated paragraphs: The "What We Do" section on the homepage had a paragraph that restated the hero copy word for word. Forbidden pattern: content that exists only to restate what was just said.

Performative honesty: "We'll tell you honestly" and "We'd rather tell you upfront than waste your time." If you have to announce that you're being honest, it reads as defensive. Just be honest.

Every fix was specific. Not "make it sound more casual" but "replace this em dash with a period" and "contract this verb." The voice profile turns subjective editorial judgment into a checklist.

The Replicable Pattern

This isn't specific to Lucas or to Briu. Any business can build a voice profile from sources they already have.

  • Build the corpus. Pull from every source where the founder or key voice actually writes: sent emails (100-200 is plenty), chat messages, documents, meeting transcripts. Skip anything that was AI-assisted.
  • Analyze for patterns. Consistent habits (sentence length, punctuation, contractions). Signature phrases. What's absent (your forbidden list). How voice changes by audience. How it's evolved over time.
  • Build the two-file system. Compact guide (~100-150 lines) for every drafting context. Full reference (~250-300 lines) for unfamiliar territory.
  • Audit existing content. Run the profile against your website, email templates, agent output. Flag every line that violates the profile. The fixes will be specific and mechanical, not subjective.
  • Maintain. Review quarterly. New patterns emerge. Old ones fade. The forbidden list grows as you find new AI-isms creeping in.

What It Can't Do

The voice profile catches mechanical patterns. It can't catch judgment. It can tell the agent to use "That said," instead of "However," but it can't tell the agent when a counterpoint is appropriate. It can enforce contractions and kill em dashes, but it can't decide whether a message needs to be warm or direct.

The human still makes the calls that require taste. The voice profile handles everything else, so the human's review time goes toward judgment instead of copy-editing.
