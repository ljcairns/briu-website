Abstract

Earth-Mars communication is constrained by physics rather than infrastructure. One-way light time varies from roughly 4 to 24 minutes depending on orbital position, which means even a short back-and-forth conversation can stretch into hours. NASA and its partners have spent decades improving the transport layer for disrupted, long-delay links through Delay-Tolerant Networking (DTN), the Bundle Protocol, and related relay and optical communication systems, but those advances primarily improve delivery and throughput rather than the number of conversational turns a human task requires [1][2][3][4].

Lightlag is proposed as an application-layer protocol that sits above BPv7 and packages each human message with predictive context expansions, likely follow-up branches, supporting data, and editable machine-generated scaffolding. The goal is not to defeat latency but to make each transmission semantically denser, allowing a multi-turn exchange to converge in fewer round trips. The protocol combines predictive context expansion, iterative convergence through human correction, retroactive editing of prior machine assumptions, and blackout delegation for conjunction periods when Earth and Mars cannot communicate at all.

Keywords: delay-tolerant networking, Bundle Protocol, interplanetary internet, Mars communications, human-in-the-loop AI, predictive context expansion
02 — Introduction

The latency problem is conversational, not merely infrastructural

The latency problem

For human crews on Mars, the central communication constraint is not whether a message can be delivered but how long it takes to complete a line of reasoning. A troubleshooting session, care escalation, procurement clarification, or personal exchange usually depends on several turns of clarification. When every round trip can cost tens of minutes, the normal structure of conversation breaks down. A protocol that treats each message as an isolated payload leaves humans paying the latency cost over and over again.

Why bandwidth alone does not solve it

Recent optical demonstrations underline the difference between throughput and interaction. In April 2024, NASA's Deep Space Optical Communications experiment transmitted test data from deep space at 267 megabits per second, showing that very high-rate space links are becoming practical [1]. That matters for imagery, logs, software updates, and science return. It does not change the speed of light. If a maintenance decision requires six clarifying questions, more bandwidth still leaves the crew waiting on six delayed exchanges. The hard problem is semantic compression of conversation, not just transport efficiency.

Current state of DTN and the Bundle Protocol

The current state of the art in challenged networking is the DTN family of protocols: the DTN architecture in RFC 4838, the Licklider Transmission Protocol in RFC 5326 for long-delay convergence, Bundle Protocol Version 7 in RFC 9171, and Bundle Protocol Security in RFC 9172 [3][4][5][6]. NASA has also flown DTN-related capabilities on the International Space Station, moving the field from theory into operational practice [2]. These protocols solve routing, custody, fragmentation, and secure delivery across interrupted links. They do not attempt to reason about the content of the message itself. Lightlag is intended to complement that stack rather than replace it.

03 — Related Work

Relevant foundations in interplanetary networking and adaptive routing

NASA DTN and operational flight work

NASA's DTN program established the core model for communication in intermittently connected environments: store, carry, and forward rather than assume an end-to-end path exists at transmission time. Work on the ISS showed how these ideas could move from lab prototypes into flight systems and staged the transition toward higher-rate deep space networking [2][7]. Lightlag assumes those transport capabilities exist and asks a narrower question: once a bundle arrives, how much unresolved conversation should still remain?

Vint Cerf's Interplanetary Internet

The Interplanetary Internet framing by Cerf, Hooke, Torgerson, Burleigh, and others remains the clearest conceptual ancestor for this work. Their contribution was to reject terrestrial assumptions about persistence, symmetry, and low latency, and to articulate a protocol approach designed for scheduled contacts and deep-space delay [8]. Lightlag extends that logic upward into the application layer: if transport assumptions must change for space, conversational assumptions likely must change as well.

DTNRG and IETF RFCs

The DTN Research Group and the later IETF standards process define the substrate on which Lightlag would operate. RFC 4838 outlines the DTN architecture. RFC 5326 defines LTP. RFC 9171 standardizes BPv7. RFC 9172 standardizes BPSec [3][4][5][6]. Those documents treat payloads as transport objects. Lightlag treats them as structured opportunities for pre-resolving future turns.

AI and machine learning applied to DTN routing

NASA-related research has already explored adaptive and machine-learned approaches to DTN routing. Dudukovich, Papachristou, Hallstrom, and Thompson proposed a machine-learning concept for DTN routing in 2017, explicitly aiming at dynamic contact opportunities and resilience in challenged environments [9]. Dudukovich's later dissertation extended this line of work into reinforcement-learning strategies for spacecraft-oriented DTN systems [10]. Francis, Vazquez-Castro, Ramamurthy, and Basu applied cognitive routing ideas to DTN-enabled space gateway services in swarm topologies [11].

That body of work is important because it shows AI being used at the network decision layer. Lightlag addresses a different but adjacent layer. Instead of asking which path a message should take, it asks what additional context should travel with the message so that fewer delayed exchanges are needed after delivery.

04 — Proposed Protocol

Lightlag as an application-layer protocol above BPv7

Lightlag is proposed as a semantic layer that wraps human-authored messages in a machine-generated context envelope before those messages enter the existing DTN stack. The protocol is designed around four operating mechanisms.

Predictive context expansion
Before transmission, the sender-side agent generates likely follow-up questions, draft answers, relevant documents, state snapshots, and confidence scores. The intent is to ship not just the immediate message but the near future of the conversation.
Iterative convergence through human-in-the-loop correction
The receiver reviews the machine expansions, accepts useful ones, rejects inaccurate ones, and adds missing context. Those corrections become part of the next outbound bundle so both sides converge on a more accurate shared state rather than continuing to drift.
Retroactive editing
Prior assumptions can be corrected after the fact. Instead of treating each turn as immutable, Lightlag allows later bundles to carry diffs against earlier machine inferences so the conversation history itself becomes editable and progressively cleaned up.
Blackout delegation
Ahead of conjunction or scheduled blackout, Earth-side operators can delegate bounded decision authority, escalation rules, and contingency playbooks to a local Mars-side agent. During the blackout, the local system acts within that scope and queues a reconciliation log for the first post-blackout contact window.

Blackout delegation model

Solar conjunction is the clearest case for delegation. If a Mars settlement expects a complete communication blackout, then a high-latency conversation protocol is still not enough. Lightlag therefore includes a handoff mode in which the Earth side transmits bounded authority scopes, escalation thresholds, and reconciliation rules before the blackout window starts. The Mars-side agent is not a free actor; it is a constrained delegate with logged authority and a mandatory post-blackout audit trail.

06 — Future Work

Validation path from orbit to Mars

ISS testing

The first useful testbed is artificial latency injection around crew-support workflows already resembling delayed operational communication. The ISS matters because NASA has already used it as a DTN proving ground [2][7]. Lightlag could be evaluated there without pretending that LEO is Mars; the point would be workflow realism, not identical signal delay.

Lunar Gateway integration

Gateway provides a nearer operational environment in which communication remains usable but no longer feels instantaneous. That is a useful middle ground: enough delay to expose friction, enough access to iterate safely. A Gateway deployment would also test how Lightlag behaves when mission operations, international partners, and higher-value procedural traffic all share the same communications environment.

Mars deployment

Mars is the actual target environment. A production deployment would need to integrate with relay scheduling, local crew interfaces, mission logs, medical escalation, and blackout governance during conjunction. The deeper question is not whether a language model can generate plausible branches. It is whether a human crew would trust the protocol enough to rely on it when the cost of an extra round trip is measured in lost hours.
