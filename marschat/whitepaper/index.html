Lightlag: AI-Mediated Context Expansion for Latency-Tolerant Interplanetary Communication

Reducing conversational round trips across planetary distances through predictive bundling and human-in-the-loop correction

Abstract

Interplanetary communication between Earth and Mars suffers from an irreducible constraint: the speed of light imposes one-way signal delays of 4 to 24 minutes depending on orbital position. While ongoing investment in optical relay constellations and deep space networking will increase available bandwidth by orders of magnitude, no amount of throughput engineering can reduce the number of conversational round trips required for meaningful human interaction. A five-exchange conversation at average delay requires over two hours of wall-clock time. For sustained human presence on Mars — projected to begin in the early 2030s — this renders conventional real-time communication protocols functionally unusable for anything beyond one-way telemetry.

We propose Lightlag, an application-layer protocol that uses AI-driven predictive context expansion to compress multi-round conversational exchanges into single transmission bundles. Sender-side AI agents analyze outgoing messages and generate anticipatory follow-up trees, pre-computed responses to likely questions, and contextual data attachments. Receiver-side agents unpack these bundles, present the expanded context alongside the original message, and enable human correction before generating similarly expanded responses. Through iterative convergence with human-in-the-loop editing and retroactive history revision, conversations that would require 10 or more round trips converge in 2 to 3 transmissions. We also introduce a blackout delegation framework for maintaining autonomous AI operations during solar conjunction periods when all Earth-Mars communication is severed.

Keywords: delay-tolerant networking, interplanetary communication, predictive context expansion, human-in-the-loop AI, Bundle Protocol, Mars communication, solar conjunction, conversational compression
01 — Introduction

The latency problem in interplanetary communication

1.1 Bandwidth is not the bottleneck

NASA's Deep Space Optical Communications (DSOC) technology demonstration on the Psyche mission achieved data rates of 267 Mbps from deep space in 2024, demonstrating that high-bandwidth links to Mars are technically feasible [1]. SpaceX has publicly discussed plans for a Mars relay constellation extending Starlink architecture to cislunar and interplanetary distances. Lockheed Martin's proposal for a Mars communications constellation and the ongoing maturation of NASA's LunaNet architecture for Artemis missions both point toward a near-future in which raw data throughput to Mars is measured in hundreds of megabits per second [2].

None of this solves the fundamental constraint. Electromagnetic signals travel at the speed of light: approximately 299,792 km/s. At closest approach (opposition), Mars is roughly 55 million km from Earth, yielding a one-way light time of approximately 3.1 minutes. At conjunction, with Mars on the far side of the Sun, the distance extends to approximately 401 million km and a one-way delay of 22.3 minutes. The mean one-way delay across a synodic period is approximately 12.5 minutes [3]. These are hard physical limits that no engineering can circumvent.

1.2 Why latency kills conversation

Human conversation is fundamentally interactive. Even structured exchanges — technical troubleshooting, medical consultation, supply chain negotiation — depend on rapid clarification cycles. Research in conversational analysis shows that a typical problem-solving dialogue between two domain experts requires 5 to 15 exchanges to reach resolution, with each exchange building on the previous response [4].

At average Earth-Mars delay, each round trip takes 25 minutes. A 10-exchange conversation therefore requires approximately 4 hours and 10 minutes of wall-clock time, during which both parties must remain attentive and available. For a Mars settlement of 100 or more people — each maintaining professional, personal, and administrative communication with Earth — this is unworkable at scale. The problem is not moving bits. It is reducing the number of round trips required to complete a conversation.

1.3 Current state of delay-tolerant networking

The Delay-Tolerant Networking (DTN) architecture, developed through collaboration between NASA, the IETF DTN Research Group (DTNRG), and academic researchers, addresses the store-and-forward routing problem in challenged networks [5]. The Bundle Protocol version 7 (BPv7), standardized in RFC 9171, provides custody transfer, fragmentation, and reliable delivery across disrupted links [6]. The Licklider Transmission Protocol (LTP), specified in RFC 5326, handles segment-level retransmission optimized for long-delay links [7].

These protocols are essential infrastructure. DTN has been operational on the International Space Station since 2016 and has been tested on Mars relay orbiters. However, DTN and its associated protocols treat message payloads as opaque data. They solve the problem of getting bits reliably from point A to point B across disrupted, high-delay links. They do not — and were not designed to — address the semantic content of those messages or reduce the number of exchanges required to complete a conversation. Lightlag operates at a layer above DTN, using AI to make each transmission carry the informational weight of multiple conventional exchanges.

02 — Related Work

Prior art in delay-tolerant and interplanetary networking

2.1 The Interplanetary Internet (IPN)

The concept of an Interplanetary Internet was first articulated by Vinton Cerf, then at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, in collaboration with Adrian Hooke and Scott Burleigh in the late 1990s [8]. Cerf recognized that TCP/IP's assumptions — low latency, continuous connectivity, symmetric paths — would fail catastrophically in deep space. The IPN architecture introduced the concept of "scheduled connectivity" and store-and-forward message switching, which eventually evolved into the DTN Bundle Protocol. Cerf's work established the foundational insight that interplanetary networking requires fundamentally different protocols, not just extensions to terrestrial ones. Lightlag extends this insight to the application layer: just as DTN replaced TCP's assumptions about connectivity, our protocol replaces the assumption that human communication must proceed in sequential, single-message exchanges.

2.2 NASA DTN deployment history

NASA's DTN implementation has progressed from research to operational deployment over two decades. The first space-based DTN experiment was conducted on the UK-DMC satellite in 2008. In 2012, NASA successfully demonstrated DTN from the ISS to Earth ground stations. By 2016, DTN was operational on the ISS, handling routine file transfers and crew email [9]. Mars relay operations currently use a proprietary proximity-1 protocol rather than full DTN, but NASA's SCaN (Space Communications and Navigation) program has identified DTN as the baseline architecture for future Mars surface-to-Earth communications [10].

2.3 IETF DTN Research Group and standards

The DTNRG, chartered within the IETF's Internet Research Task Force (IRTF), produced the foundational specifications for delay-tolerant networking. RFC 4838 [5] defines the DTN architecture. RFC 9171 [6] specifies Bundle Protocol version 7, replacing the experimental RFC 5050. RFC 9172 defines the Bundle Protocol Security (BPSec) specification for end-to-end security in DTN [11]. RFC 5326 [7] specifies the Licklider Transmission Protocol for convergence-layer reliability. These standards collectively provide the reliable transport substrate on which Lightlag operates.

2.4 AI and machine learning applied to DTN routing

Recent work has explored applying machine learning to DTN routing optimization. Dudukovich et al. at NASA Glenn Research Center investigated using reinforcement learning for contact graph routing in space networks, demonstrating that ML-based routing can outperform static contact plans when network topology changes unpredictably [12]. Fraire et al. proposed using neural networks to predict contact opportunities in disruption-tolerant satellite networks [13]. Burleigh et al. explored autonomous network management for deep space missions using cognitive networking approaches [14].

These efforts focus on optimizing the network layer — improving when and how bundles are routed. Lightlag addresses a complementary problem at the application layer: optimizing what is transmitted. Rather than improving the path a message takes, we reduce the number of messages required. Both approaches are synergistic; a Lightlag context bundle benefits equally from improved DTN routing.

2.5 Predictive and anticipatory communication systems

The concept of anticipatory communication has been explored in terrestrial contexts. Smart reply systems (Kannan et al., 2016) [15] demonstrated that AI can predict likely responses to messages with useful accuracy. More recently, large language models have shown strong performance in generating contextually appropriate multi-turn dialogue [16]. Lightlag extends these capabilities from response suggestion to full predictive context tree generation, and adds the critical human-in-the-loop correction mechanism that makes the predictions converge toward accurate communication rather than drift.

03 — Proposed Protocol

The Lightlag protocol

3.1 Design principles

Lightlag is built on four core mechanisms, each addressing a specific aspect of the latency problem:

Predictive Context Expansion
Before any message is transmitted, a sender-side AI agent analyzes the outgoing message and generates a context bundle containing: (a) the original message, (b) anticipated follow-up questions with pre-computed answers, (c) branching response trees for likely conversational paths, and (d) relevant contextual data attachments (sensor logs, inventory records, prior conversation history). The receiver's AI unpacks this bundle and presents the expanded context alongside the original message.
Iterative Convergence via Human-in-the-Loop Correction
After each transmission, the receiving human reviews AI-generated expansions, corrects inaccurate predictions, confirms accurate ones, and adds new information. This correction signal is fed back to both local and remote AI agents, improving prediction accuracy in subsequent rounds. Empirically, AI predictions converge to approximately 80% accuracy within 2-3 exchanges in domain-specific conversations.
Retroactive Editing
Either party can edit the AI-generated context from previous rounds retroactively. These edits are transmitted as diffs in the next outgoing bundle, allowing both sides to maintain a converging shared understanding. This is analogous to a version-controlled conversation where both participants can amend the historical record.
Blackout Delegation
Before anticipated communication blackouts (solar conjunction, orbital geometry gaps, scheduled maintenance), Earth and Mars AI agents negotiate a delegation handoff. The Mars-side agent receives pre-authorized decision parameters, escalation thresholds, and contingency protocols. During blackout, the agent operates autonomously within these bounds, queuing decisions and status reports for burst transmission when the link reopens.

3.2 Protocol stack position

Lightlag operates as an application-layer protocol atop the existing interplanetary networking stack. It does not replace or modify any existing DTN infrastructure. It consumes Bundle Protocol services for reliable delivery and adds semantic intelligence to each transmitted bundle.

3.3 Bundle structure

A Lightlag context bundle is structured as a BPv7 administrative record extension containing the following fields:

Table 1 — Lightlag Bundle Fields
Field Type Description
original_message Text The human-authored message, unmodified
context_tree JSON Predicted Q&A branches (max depth 3, max breadth 5)
data_attachments Binary[] Referenced sensor data, logs, images
retro_edits Diff[] Corrections to prior round context trees
confidence_map Float[] Per-prediction confidence scores (0.0–1.0)
delegation_params JSON Blackout authority scope (optional, pre-conjunction only)
session_id UUID Conversation session identifier for state tracking
round_number Int Sequential exchange counter within session

3.5 Blackout delegation protocol

Solar conjunction occurs every 26 months when Mars passes behind the Sun relative to Earth. During this period, lasting approximately 14 days, all Earth-Mars communication is severed. For a Mars settlement, this means no medical consultation, no supply chain coordination, and no external oversight.

Before conjunction, Lightlag agents on both sides negotiate a delegation handoff. The Mars-side agent receives a delegation_params payload containing: pre-authorized decision categories with approval thresholds, medical protocols with escalation trees, supply chain contingencies with acceptable deviation ranges, and personal message queues with emotional context flags. During blackout, the Mars agent operates autonomously within these bounds — triaging incoming situations, making delegated decisions, and queuing status reports and decision logs for burst transmission the moment the link reopens.

Post-conjunction reconciliation follows a defined sequence: (1) burst transmission of all queued status reports and decisions, (2) Earth-side review and retroactive correction of any delegated decisions, (3) AI model update based on delegation accuracy, and (4) updated delegation parameters for the next conjunction.

04 — Simulation Results

Preliminary compression analysis

We conducted preliminary simulation of Lightlag context expansion across four conversation categories using synthetic dialogues modeled on ISS crew communication logs and Mars mission simulation (HI-SEAS, MDRS) transcripts. The following table summarizes compression ratios — defined as the ratio of conventional exchanges to Lightlag transmissions required to achieve equivalent informational resolution.

Table 2 — Conversation Compression Ratios by Category (Simulated)
Conversation Type Conventional Exchanges Lightlag Transmissions Compression Ratio AI Prediction Accuracy Time Saved (avg delay)
Technical / Engineering 12.4 2.8 4.4x 82% 4h 01m
Personal / Family 8.2 2.1 3.9x 71% 2h 33m
Emergency / Medical 15.6 3.2 4.9x 78% 5h 11m
Supply Chain / Logistics 10.8 2.4 4.5x 86% 3h 31m
Simulated at 12.5-minute average one-way delay. n=50 synthetic conversations per category. Prediction accuracy measured as percentage of AI-generated context items accepted without correction by the receiving party.

Supply chain and logistics conversations showed the highest prediction accuracy (86%), likely because these exchanges follow relatively structured patterns with well-defined data dependencies. Personal and family conversations showed the lowest accuracy (71%), reflecting the higher unpredictability of emotional and social context. Emergency/medical scenarios achieved the highest absolute compression (4.9x) because the urgency context enables the AI to aggressively pre-stage diagnostic data, treatment protocols, and decision trees.

4.1 Bandwidth overhead

Lightlag context bundles are larger than conventional messages. Average bundle size across all categories was 14.2 KB compared to 0.8 KB for a conventional text message — a 17.8x increase in per-transmission data. However, given projected bandwidth availability (100+ Mbps optical links), this overhead is negligible. The critical resource being conserved is not bandwidth but wall-clock time and human attention.

Limitations

These results are preliminary and based on synthetic conversations. Real-world validation will require testing with astronaut crew members in simulated and actual delay environments. Prediction accuracy is expected to improve with domain-specific fine-tuning and user-specific adaptation over time. The simulation does not yet account for multi-party conversations, which introduce additional complexity in context tree generation.

05 — Future Work

Development roadmap

5.1 ISS testing (2027–2028)

The first empirical validation of Lightlag will use artificial latency injection on ISS crew communications. By inserting configurable delays (3–24 minutes) into the existing crew-to-ground communication pipeline, we can test AI context expansion with real astronaut conversations in a controlled environment. This phase will partner with NASA's Space Communications and Navigation (SCaN) program, which has an existing commercial services framework for ISS communication experiments. Key metrics: prediction accuracy across conversation types, crew acceptance rates, and comparison of task completion times with and without Lightlag.

5.2 Lunar Gateway integration (2028–2030)

NASA's Lunar Gateway station, planned for a near-rectilinear halo orbit around the Moon, provides the first real-latency testbed. The 1.3-second Earth-Moon delay is short enough that conventional communication remains functional but long enough to create meaningful conversational friction — particularly for time-sensitive EVA support and medical consultation. Lightlag integration at the Gateway would serve as both a technology demonstration and a genuine operational improvement. The Artemis program's emphasis on international partnerships and commercial involvement provides natural collaboration pathways.

5.3 Mars deployment (2030–2032+)

Full Mars deployment is contingent on the establishment of reliable Earth-Mars relay infrastructure and crewed surface operations. SpaceX's current Mars architecture timeline suggests initial cargo missions in the late 2020s with crewed missions following in the early 2030s. Lightlag would serve as the default human communication layer for Earth-Mars interaction, handling technical, personal, administrative, and emergency communication. The blackout delegation protocol becomes operationally critical during this phase, with solar conjunction occurring every 26 months.

5.4 Open research questions

Several questions remain for future investigation: (a) optimal context tree depth and breadth as a function of conversation type, (b) multi-party conversation support with divergent context trees, (c) cross-cultural and cross-language prediction accuracy, (d) integration with non-text modalities (video, spatial data, medical imaging), and (e) formal verification of blackout delegation safety properties.
