01 — The Problem

Bandwidth is not the bottleneck. Latency is.

NASA's Deep Space Optical Communications (DSOC) experiment on the Psyche mission demonstrated laser links at 267 Mbps from deep space. SpaceX, Lockheed Martin, and Blue Origin are all building commercial Mars relay constellations. By 2032, bandwidth to Mars will be measured in hundreds of megabits per second.

None of that solves the fundamental constraint: the speed of light cannot be engineered away. At closest approach, a message takes 4 minutes one way. At opposition, 24 minutes. Average: 13 minutes. A simple "yes or no?" question has a 26-minute round trip. A typical mission control interaction that requires 5 exchanges takes over two hours. For a colony of 100+ people trying to talk to families on Earth, this is unworkable.

Current protocols (NASA's DTN, Bundle Protocol v7) solve store-and-forward routing. They get bits from A to B reliably across disrupted links. But they treat every message as opaque cargo. They have no understanding of what's being communicated, and therefore no ability to reduce the number of round trips needed to complete a conversation.

02 — The Protocol

Predictive context expansion with human-in-the-loop correction

The core idea: before any message is transmitted, an AI agent on the sender's side expands it into a context bundle containing the original message, anticipated follow-up questions, pre-computed answers to those questions, and branching response trees for the most likely conversational paths. The receiver's AI unpacks this, presents the original message alongside the expanded context, and the receiver edits, corrects, and responds. Their response is similarly expanded before transmission.

After each round trip, both parties can review the AI-generated expansions and edit history retroactively. This creates an iterative convergence process: the AI fills in blanks, humans correct, and within 3 to 5 actual transmissions the conversation achieves the informational density of 20 to 30 conventional exchanges.

03 — Architecture

Where AI fits in the interplanetary protocol stack

Lightlag operates as an application-layer protocol that sits on top of NASA's existing DTN/Bundle Protocol infrastructure. It doesn't replace any of the work being done on physical relay, store-and-forward routing, or bandwidth optimization. It makes all of that infrastructure dramatically more useful for human communication.

▸ Lightlag Protocol AI context expansion, predictive bundling, retroactive editing
Application Layer Messaging, video, file transfer, telemetry
Bundle Protocol (BPv7) Store-and-forward, custody transfer, fragmentation
Convergence Layer TCP, LTP, UDP adaptation
Link Layer RF (DSN, UHF), Optical (DSOC, Starlink-Mars)
Physical Layer Relay orbiters (MRO, MAVEN, TGO), ground stations, laser terminals
↑ This is the gap. Everything below exists. Everything above is what we're building.

06 — The Blackout Problem

What does an AI agent do during a 14-day solar conjunction?

Every 26 months, Mars passes behind the Sun from Earth's perspective. For roughly two weeks, all communication is severed. No messages in or out. For a colony, this means no medical consultation, no supply chain coordination, no family contact.

A Lightlag agent on Mars during conjunction operates with full delegated authority within pre-approved parameters. Before conjunction begins, both Earth and Mars agents negotiate a context handoff: pending decisions with approval thresholds, medical protocols with escalation trees, supply chain contingencies, personal message queues with emotional context flags. The Mars-side agent triages, decides, and queues status reports for burst transmission the moment the link reopens.

This is the same problem every business faces when a decision-maker is unreachable. The Mars version is just the most extreme case. The protocol we're building here applies anywhere autonomous AI agents need to operate independently while maintaining alignment with human intent across communication gaps.
