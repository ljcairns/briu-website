The Wake-Up Call

This is not about replacing your team.
It's about what your team becomes.

In early 2026, investor and entrepreneur Jason Calacanis deployed AI agents across his 20-person venture firm. Not to cut headcount. To find out what happens when every employee has an AI counterpart working alongside them.

He started with the most technical staff and worked his way down. Each person got their own agent. Each agent got its own email, Slack, and CRM accounts. He calls them "replicants." A master agent manages the others, checks their work, and summarizes everything daily. The goal was not automation for its own sake. It was leverage.

"I'm going down the slope of employees from most technical to least and trying to get each one of them to adopt and create an agent. It will probably take six months, but when we do, I think our leverage versus a competing firm is going to be 10x."

Jason Calacanis on his OpenClaw deployment — All-In Podcast E261

Within the first few weeks, the results were clear:

"Out of 50 hours a producer does a week, this does 40 of them. And of what an SDR does, this does 95%."

Jason Calacanis on his OpenClaw deployment — All-In Podcast E259

"On the average investment team individual, we now have probably 20% of their work being done by agents in real time."

Jason Calacanis on his OpenClaw deployment — All-In Podcast E261

His assistant's workload was reduced by 30%. Not to cut her hours. To free her up for higher-level work. The agents took the repetitive tasks. The humans moved up.

Your employees become agent orchestrators

David Sacks, co-host and former PayPal COO, made a prediction at the start of 2026 that runs counter to most AI headlines:

"My most contrarian belief is that AI would increase demand for knowledge workers, not put them out of business."

David Sacks on AI agents — All-In Podcast E261

His reasoning is simple. Employees who adopt these tools do not do less work. They do more, at a higher level. The routine tasks move to agents. The thinking, the judgment, the relationships stay with people.

"A key skill of employees is going to be the ability to structure work for themselves and their AI agents. And the employees who can do that are going to be far more productive than those who can't."

David Sacks on AI agents — All-In Podcast E261

"Employees who are early adopters of these tools are going to appear to have superpowers. They're going to be the people in meetings who can take an assignment that would have taken days and get it done in 2 hours."

David Sacks on AI agents — All-In Podcast E261

This is the real transition. Not AI replacing people. People learning to orchestrate AI.

It starts simple: an agent that reads your email and prepares a morning briefing. Then it drafts responses for your review. Then it manages your CRM. Then it runs overnight, researching prospects and preparing reports so your team wakes up to work already done. Each step builds trust. Each step builds skill.

Your employees go from using AI as a search tool to managing a team of agents that handles the operational load of your business. The businesses doing this now are not just more efficient. They are building a workforce that understands how to structure work for AI. That capability is compounding. And it is not something you can hire for yet.

"Every startup I know is hiring for this position, which is somebody who knows how to build and manage agents. There is no job rec for this yet or a title."

Jason Calacanis on his OpenClaw deployment — All-In Podcast E261

The cost question

None of this is free. Here are the real numbers from production.

"We hit $300 a day per agent using the Claude API, instantly. And that was doing maybe 10 or 20%. That's $100,000 a year per agent."

Jason Calacanis on his OpenClaw deployment — All-In Podcast E261

Mark Cuban responded to these numbers publicly. He called them "the smartest counter I've seen to AI taking over jobs, in the short term." His math: if eight agents at $300/day each plus maintenance cost more than the employee they support, the agents need to be over twice as productive just to break even. He compared current agents to "college interns that come in hungover, make mistakes, and don't take responsibility for them."

He is asking the right question. But he is treating agents as an operating expense. You pay for them, they do work, the money is consumed.

That is the wrong frame.

Every dollar builds something permanent

The agent is not just doing today's work. It is learning your business.

Every email it triages teaches it your communication patterns. Every CRM update teaches it your deal flow. Every prospecting run teaches it your ideal customer. That knowledge is permanent. It does not leave when an employee quits. It does not need to be retrained when you hire someone new. It does not forget over the weekend.

This is not an operating cost. It is a capital investment. You are building infrastructure that compounds in value every day it runs. And unlike physical infrastructure, it does not depreciate. It appreciates.

"Every time you add it to something, it gets smarter."

Jason Calacanis on his OpenClaw deployment — All-In Podcast E259

"They don't forget to do work. They don't make mistakes. Once you put this in, you don't need to have checklists. They just do it perfectly every single time."

Jason Calacanis on his OpenClaw deployment — All-In Podcast E261

You do not need to spend $300 a day

That is what it costs to run agents at full capacity around the clock across an entire team. That is where the early adopters with venture-scale budgets are spending.

Most businesses do not start there. And they should not.

We run a fully capable AI agent managing two email accounts, a CRM, sales prospecting, automated client reporting, calendar, and cost monitoring. Our daily cost: $2 to $5. Not because we cut corners. Because we built the architecture to be efficient. A personal agent that orchestrates task-specific agents. Each one given a defined job, a defined budget, and a defined scope. They run when there is work to do and stop when it is done.

The progression for most businesses looks like this:

$2-5/day

Read, learn, brief

Agent connects to email, calendar, CRM with read-only access. Morning briefings in Slack or Discord. Your team sees what it can do.
No write access. No send permissions.
$5-15/day

Draft and suggest

Agent drafts email responses, prepares CRM updates, flags stale deals. Everything waits for your review. You approve, edit, or reject.
Every action requires explicit approval.
$15-30/day

Approve and execute

Approval gates go live. Agent proposes, you tap a button, it executes. Draft, review, send. Your team builds trust on your schedule.
Approval buttons with full audit logging.
$30+/day

Let it ride

Low-risk actions auto-execute. High-stakes still gated. Overnight prospecting, reporting, follow-ups. Your team wakes up to work already done.
Budget ceilings. Daily alerts. Revoke anytime.

The $300/day fully autonomous setup is a destination, not a starting point. And by the time you get there, the cost to run it will be significantly lower than it is today.

What happens if you wait

This is not a warning about AI taking over. It is a straightforward competitive reality.

Sacks predicted that AI adoption in 2026 would not come from the top down. Not from CEO transformation initiatives. Not from enterprise RFP processes that take six months. It would come from individual employees who figured it out themselves.

"You're going to see massive enterprise adoption of AI this year. But it's going to be driven by the bottom up, by these early adopter employees bringing in consumerized AI tools."

David Sacks on AI agents — All-In Podcast E261

Somewhere right now, a business that competes with yours has an employee figuring this out. They are building an agent that handles their inbox. They are automating their CRM updates. They are running prospect research overnight. And they are getting better at it every week.

The businesses that start now get three advantages that compound over time:

1
Institutional knowledge. Every workflow your agents learn is permanent. Six months of agent learning is six months your competitor has to make up. That gap does not close by itself.
2
A team that knows how to work with AI. Your employees build the skill of structuring work for agents. That capability comes from months of working alongside the tools, not from a training seminar.
3
A cost curve in your favor. The infrastructure you build today runs on tomorrow's better, cheaper models with no migration required. You are locked into today's learning, not today's pricing.

You do not need to wait for a big corporation to build this for you. The framework is open source. One developer built it using an AI coding agent. You build what your business needs, you own it, and you start getting value this week.
